Introduction

  • according to the Bronsted-Lowry definition, acids are proton (hydrogen ion (H+)) donors
    • produce hydronium (H3O+) ions in solution
    • acids (acidity itself) can affect the appearance of an indicator
  • hydrochloric acid is not acidic by itself, per se - it reacts with water to form hydronium ion + chlorine ion. the former is what causes the acidity.
    • dative (aka donor) bond formed between H+ and H2O, where the oxygen supplies one of its lone pairs to form a bond with the hydrogen.
  • higher concentration of H+ ions in solution mean high acid strength/higher acidity.
    • this is reflective of the acid's bond strength

Polyprotic Acids

  • Monoprotic acids can donate one proton only (e.g HCl, hydrochloric acid)
  • Diprotic acids can donate two protons (e.g H2SO4, sulfuric acid (aq))
  • Triprotic acids can donate three protons (e.g H3PO4, phosphoric acid)

Ionisation

  • Ionisation refers to the dissociation of a covalent molecule to form ions, when reacting with water (in solution) e.g
    HCl(-) + H2O ⇌ H3O + Cl(-)
    this reaction is almost 100% ionised (as HCl is incredibly strong); hence, it is an excellent conductor.

Acid Strength and Concentration

  • The strongest proton donors are the strongest acids -> this means they dissociate well in solution, producing a relatively high concentration of hydronium ions.
  • The strongest acids have the weakest conjugate bases, and vice versa.
  • Not to be confused with concentrated/dilute substances: this refers to the proportion of water compared to the substance in a solution.

Ionisation vs Dissociation

  • Ionisation refers to ionic compounds in particular (in this context, usually happens with acids)
  • Dissociation refers to covalent compounds (in this context, usually happens with bases)
  • e.g the dissociation reaction for BaCl2 + aq -> Ba(2+) + 2Cl(-)
